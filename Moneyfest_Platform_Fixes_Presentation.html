Security Gap #1 · Fixed

Our borrower records were visible to anyone.

Imagine your office filing cabinet — containing every borrower's name, phone number, loan amount, payment history, and account PIN — was left unlocked in a public lobby. Anyone who walked in could open it, read everything, and even change the numbers inside.

That is exactly what our system was doing online. The records were protected by a lock that had no combination set — anyone who tried the handle could walk right in.

BEFORE

Any person on the internet could look up any borrower's personal details, loan balances, and access codes. They could also modify loan records — marking loans as paid without actually paying.

AFTER

Every borrower can now only see their own records. Admins see everything. No one else sees anything.

FIXED — April 2026
Security Gap #2 · Fixed

Our daily penalty processor had no lock on the door.

Every morning, our system runs an automatic process that checks which borrowers are late and applies ₱20/day penalties. Think of it like a daily clerk who goes through all accounts and stamps overdue notices.

The problem: that clerk's office had no door lock. Anyone who found the address could walk in and trigger the process manually — applying penalties to all borrowers, sending overdue notices to everyone, and deducting credit scores — even when no one was actually late.

BEFORE

Anyone who discovered the system address could trigger mass penalty application at any time of day or night.

AFTER

The process now requires a secret key that only our server knows. No key, no entry. External triggers are rejected immediately.

FIXED — April 2026
Security Gap #3 · Fixed

Anyone could submit, read, or modify payment records.

Our principal payment records — the logs of every peso a borrower has paid toward their loan — were stored in a folder with no access rules at all. Not even a basic lock. Anyone could read every payment ever made, submit fake payments, or modify existing payment entries without logging in.

BEFORE

Payment records were fully accessible to the public — readable, writable, and modifiable by anyone.

AFTER

Only the borrower who made the payment can submit their own record. Only admins can update or review. No one else has access.

FIXED — April 2026
Accounting Error #1 · Fixed

Our profit reports were showing money that didn't exist.

Here is what was happening with overdue borrowers: When a borrower went late, our system was supposed to deduct ₱20/day from their ₱500 security deposit. Correct so far.

But when the deposit ran out — say after 25 days — and the borrower still hadn't paid, the system kept going. It added the extra penalty on top of what the borrower owed. Fine.

The problem: it also added that extra penalty to our income records — labeling it as "Interest Profit Earned." So our books were showing ₱175 interest earned this installment. But in reality, ₱140 was real interest and ₱35 was a penalty we hadn't actually collected yet.

The borrower still owed it.

What the books showed What was actually true
₱175 Interest Earned ₱140 Real + ₱35 Uncollected
₱700 profit this cycle ₱560 confirmed + ₱140 pending
AFTER Penalties are no longer mixed into interest income. Only confirmed collected cash is logged as profit.
Accounting Error #2 · Fixed

When one loan went overdue, all of a borrower's loans were affected.

When a borrower has two loans — say Loan A is overdue and Loan B is being paid on time — our system was supposed to mark only Loan A as "credit penalty applied."

Instead, it was marking BOTH loans. So when the borrower later paid Loan B on time, the system saw the flag and skipped the credit score improvement they had earned. Even worse, it could prevent the overdue warning from being re-applied if Loan A got worse.

"It was like putting a 'delinquent' sticker on the wrong file folder."

BEFORE

One late loan caused flags to appear on ALL of that borrower's loans — including ones paid perfectly on time.

AFTER

The flag is now applied to the exact loan that is late. Other loans are completely unaffected.

Accounting Error #3 · Fixed

Borrowers who paid on time were being charged a late penalty.

Our payment due dates are the 5th and 20th of every month. A borrower who paid on May 5th was doing everything right.

But our system was reading dates in a different timezone — the international standard time — which meant midnight on May 5th in the Philippines was being recorded as 11:59pm on May 4th internationally.

Result: the system thought the payment came in one day late. It automatically charged ₱20 penalty. It deducted 10 credit score points. All for a borrower who paid exactly on time.

BEFORE

On-time payments on the 5th or 20th were being recorded as 1 day late. ₱20 wrongful penalty + 10 credit score points wrongfully deducted.

AFTER

All dates now use Philippine local time. An on-time payment is recorded as on time. Always.

System Crash #1 · Fixed

Borrowers could not cash out their rebate credits. Ever.

When a borrower earns rebate credits — from paying early, completing their loan, or receiving their security deposit back — those credits sit in their account wallet.

When they request a withdrawal of those credits, an admin reviews and approves the request. Simple process.

Except: every single time an admin clicked Approve, the system crashed silently in the background. The admin saw no error. The borrower saw no result. The money never moved. The request sat there forever. This was happening because of a single mislabeled record in the approval process — like a teller who kept reaching for the wrong drawer every time.

BEFORE

100% of withdrawal approval attempts failed silently. No borrower could access their rebate credits.

AFTER

Withdrawal approvals process correctly. Borrowers receive their credits as expected.

System Crash #2 · Fixed

Confirming or rejecting payment proof crashed the admin dashboard.

When a borrower submits proof of payment — a GCash screenshot for example — the admin reviews it and clicks either Confirm or Reject.

Both buttons were broken. Every click caused a silent crash. The admin dashboard would freeze or appear to do nothing. No confirmation was sent to the borrower. No record was updated. No notification was triggered.

The borrower would be left waiting indefinitely, with no idea whether their payment was accepted.

BEFORE

Clicking Confirm or Reject on any payment proof caused an immediate crash. Borrowers never received confirmation of their payments.

AFTER

Both actions now work correctly. Borrower receives an instant portal notification upon admin decision.

The Core Accounting Problem · Fully Solved

How I Prevented Ghost Money From Corrupting Our Books.

1. What is ghost money?
Ghost money is when our accounting system records income that does not physically exist in our GCash. The books say we earned it. Our wallet says we don't have it. This has happened to us before and required manual cleanup.

2. How it was being created:

1Borrower has ₱500 security deposit held by us

↓

2Borrower goes 30+ days overdue — deposit used up

↓

3System continued adding daily penalty to our income records labeled as 'Interest Profit' — even though we hadn't collected it. The borrower still owed it.

↓
4

Books showed ₱175 profit per payment. Actual cash received: ₱140.

₱35 was ghost

3. How it was solved:

I engineered a permanent separation between penalties and interest. Interest profit is now only logged when an installment payment is physically received. Penalties are only logged when they are actually collected — not when they are applied.

The security deposit is now tracked separately from income. When it absorbs a penalty, it is recorded as a deduction from the deposit — not as income earned. Only the leftover penalty that exceeds the deposit gets logged — and only at the moment the borrower pays it.

"Our books now only show money we actually have. Not money we are owed. Not money we hope to collect. Only money in hand."

The Financial Engine

Capital Rotation & Profit Splitting

A scheduled ₱1,425 payment is not ₱1,425 of profit. Here is how our automated system splits every transaction to protect our capital pool.

Bucket A: Capital Rotation (Principal)

This is the money we originally lent out. When it comes back, it immediately returns to the 'Vault'. This money is physically rotated and scheduled to be lent out again to the next borrower to keep the business supply moving.

Bucket B: True Profit (Interest)

This is the actual fee we earned over time. Our system strictly isolates this portion from the principal. This is the only money that counts as our profit. By keeping it separate, we never accidentally cannibalize our lending capability.

How The Capital Forecast Works

Because we strictly separate Principal from Profit, our forecasting engine can perfectly predict our future lending capacity. It securely scans all active loans, plots the exact due dates of expected Principal returns, and calculates exactly how much pure capital will be available to rotate into new loans next week. This guarantees we can issue approvals to new borrowers without over-committing our GCash balance.
